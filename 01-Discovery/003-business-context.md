# 1. Introduction

Westbridge Hospitals Trust (WUHNFT) has undergone significant digital transformation to improve healthcare delivery, enhance patient experience, and support clinical decision-making.

Digital technologies now underpin almost every aspect of Trust operations, from electronic patient records and diagnostic services to patient communication platforms and research systems.

While this transformation has delivered significant benefits, it has also increased the Trust's dependency on technology and introduced new cyber security risks.

The Trust Board has therefore commissioned **Project SentinelCare – Cyber Security Governance, Risk and Compliance Improvement Programme** to establish a stronger cyber security governance framework, improve regulatory readiness, and reduce organisational cyber risk.

The programme aims to provide assurance that:

> "The organisation understands its cybersecurity risks and has appropriate controls in place to protect patients, clinical services, and sensitive information."


# 2. Strategic Business Drivers

The requirement for Project SentinelCare has been driven by several strategic factors affecting the Trust.

## 2.1 Increasing Cyber Threat Landscape

Healthcare organisations continue to be targeted by cyber criminals due to:

- The value of healthcare data.
- The critical nature of clinical services.
- The potential operational impact of disruption.
- The pressure organisations may face during ransomware incidents.

Cyber attacks against healthcare providers can affect:

- Patient safety.
- Clinical operations.
- Service availability.
- Public confidence.
- Regulatory compliance.

The Trust recognises that cyber security is a patient safety issue as well as a technology concern.


## 2.2 Digital Transformation and Increased Complexity

The Trust's digital transformation programme has introduced significant benefits but has also increased the complexity of managing cyber risk.

The current environment includes:

- Cloud-based services.
- On-premises infrastructure.
- Connected medical devices.
- Clinical applications.
- Third-party healthcare platforms.
- Patient-facing digital services.

This expanded technology landscape requires stronger governance, clearer ownership, and improved visibility of cyber risks.

---

## 2.3 Regulatory and Compliance Requirements

As an NHS healthcare provider, WUHNFT must demonstrate compliance with multiple regulatory and industry requirements.

Key obligations include:

- UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR).
- Data Protection Act 2018.
- NHS Data Security and Protection Toolkit (DSPT).
- Network and Information Systems Regulations (NIS).
- NHS information governance requirements.

Failure to meet these obligations could result in:

- Regulatory action.
- Financial penalties.
- Increased scrutiny.
- Loss of public confidence.
- Impact on healthcare delivery.

A concrete, healthcare-specific consequence is that a "Standards Not Met" outcome in the annual NHS DSP Toolkit submission can restrict a Trust's access to shared national systems (such as the NHS Spine and shared care record services), directly affecting the ability to exchange patient information with other care providers. This illustrates why cyber security governance is a direct enabler of clinical service delivery, not solely a compliance exercise.

---

## 2.4 Risk Appetite Statement

The Trust Board's cyber risk appetite is summarised as follows:

- **Low appetite** for risks that could affect patient safety, clinical service availability, or the confidentiality of patient information. Such risks are treated as priorities for mitigation rather than acceptance.
- **Moderate appetite** for risks affecting non-critical corporate functions (e.g., internal administrative systems), where a degree of residual risk may be accepted subject to documented sign-off by the relevant risk owner.
- **Low appetite** for non-compliance with statutory and regulatory obligations (UK GDPR, DSPT, NIS Regulations), reflecting the Trust's public accountability.

This statement provides the reference point against which risks recorded in the cyber risk register are scored and escalated. It is intended to be reviewed and formally approved by the Trust Board as part of programme governance, rather than treated as fixed.

## 2.5 Protection of Patient Safety

Healthcare services depend on reliable access to accurate information.

Disruption to critical systems could affect:

- Emergency care.
- Clinical decision-making.
- Medication management.
- Diagnostic services.
- Patient communication.

The Trust recognises that cyber resilience is essential to maintaining safe patient care.


# 3. Current Business Challenges

The Trust has identified several areas where cyber security maturity requires improvement.


## 3.1 Limited Cyber Risk Visibility

Current challenges include:

- No central cyber risk register.
- Inconsistent risk ownership.
- Limited visibility of cyber risk exposure.
- Difficulty reporting cyber risks consistently to senior leadership.

The Trust requires a structured approach to identify, assess, treat, and monitor cyber risks.

## 3.2 Inconsistent Security Governance

Although security policies exist, they are not consistently maintained or applied across the organisation.

Challenges include:

- Different departments following different processes.
- Policies requiring review and alignment.
- Limited evidence of control effectiveness.
- Lack of consistent governance reporting.

The Trust requires a coordinated security governance framework aligned with recognised standards.


## 3.3 Identity and Access Management Concerns

An internal review identified excessive access permissions across some systems.

Potential risks include:

- Users retaining unnecessary privileges.
- Increased insider threat exposure.
- Inadequate access reviews.
- Weak segregation of duties.

The Trust requires stronger identity governance practices including:

- Role-based access control.
- Regular access reviews.
- Privileged access management.
- Improved authentication controls.


## 3.4 Medical Device Security Challenges

The Trust operates a large number of connected medical devices supporting clinical care.

A security review identified:

- 25 devices running unsupported operating systems.
- Limited vendor patching capability.
- Incomplete ownership records.

These devices present challenges because:

- They support critical clinical services.
- They may have long operational lifecycles.
- Security updates may require vendor involvement.
- Replacement may require significant investment.

## 3.5 Third-Party Supplier Risk

WUHNFT depends on more than 150 external suppliers supporting healthcare delivery.

Examples include:

- Clinical software providers.
- Medical device manufacturers.
- Cloud service providers.
- Managed service providers.

Current challenges include:

- No central supplier security assessment process.
- Inconsistent security requirements within contracts.
- Limited visibility of supplier access.
- Difficulty monitoring supplier cyber risk.


## 3.6 Data Governance Challenges

The Trust processes large volumes of sensitive information including:

- Patient records.
- Medical images.
- Research data.
- Staff information.

An internal audit identified:

- Inconsistent data classification.
- Excessive access permissions.
- Uncontrolled copies of patient reports.
- Storage outside approved systems.

Improved information governance is required to protect confidentiality, integrity, and availability of Trust information.


# 4. Business Impact of Cyber Risk

Cyber security incidents could have significant consequences for the Trust.

## Patient Impact

Potential consequences include:

- Delayed diagnosis.
- Reduced access to clinical information.
- Disruption to treatment pathways.
- Increased patient safety risks.


## Operational Impact

Potential consequences include:

- Loss of critical systems.
- Reduced healthcare capacity.
- Increased manual processes.
- Increased recovery costs.


## Financial Impact

Potential consequences include:

- Incident response costs.
- System restoration costs.
- Regulatory penalties.
- Supplier remediation costs.


## Reputational Impact

Potential consequences include:

- Reduced patient confidence.
- Negative media attention.
- Loss of stakeholder trust.
- Increased regulatory scrutiny.


# 5. Project SentinelCare Objectives

The programme aims to strengthen cyber security governance and improve organisational resilience, addressing the business challenges set out in Section 3. The full set of objectives, baselines, and CAF outcome mapping is defined in [004-project_objectives](004-project_objectives.md).


# 6. Expected Business Outcomes

Successful delivery of Project SentinelCare will enable the Trust to:

- Understand its current cyber security maturity.
- Identify and prioritise cyber risks.
- Improve regulatory compliance.
- Strengthen security governance.
- Improve incident preparedness.
- Establish clearer security ownership.
- Improve decision-making through security metrics.
- Protect critical healthcare services.


# 7. Programme Success Criteria

The programme will be considered successful when the Trust can demonstrate:

## Governance

- Approved security policies.
- Defined security responsibilities.
- Regular security reporting.

## Risk Management

- Central cyber risk register.
- Documented risk treatment plans.
- Clear risk ownership.

## Compliance

- Improved NHS DSPT evidence readiness.
- Completed framework assessments.
- Audit traceability.

## Resilience

- Tested incident response procedures.
- Improved recovery planning.
- Documented lessons learned.

## Reporting

- Executive cyber dashboard.
- Security performance indicators.
- Improved visibility of cyber exposure.


# 8. Conclusion

Project SentinelCare represents the Trust's commitment to improving cyber security maturity and protecting healthcare services against evolving threats.

The programme recognises that effective cyber security requires a combination of:

- Strong governance.
- Risk-based decision-making.
- Regulatory compliance.
- Technical controls.
- Security awareness.
- Continuous improvement.

By strengthening its cyber security capability, WUHNFT aims to provide safer, more resilient digital healthcare services for patients, staff, and healthcare partners.
